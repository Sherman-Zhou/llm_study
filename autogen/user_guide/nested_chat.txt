### Lesson Plan: The Water Cycle

#### Standards:
- **CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RI.4.3**: Explain events, procedures, ideas, or concepts in a historical, scientific, or technical text, including what happened and when.
- **NGSS 4-ESS2-2**: Make observations to provide evidence of the effects of weathering, erosion, and deposition at various places on Earth's surface.

#### Objectives:
1. By the end of this lesson, students will be able to explain the processes of evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and collection in the water cycle.
2. By the end of this lesson, students will be able to describe the stages of the water cycle and their sequence.
3. By the end of this lesson, students will be able to illustrate the water cycle using diagrams and explain the movement of water through the environment.

#### Materials:
- Whiteboard and markers
- Water cycle diagram handouts
- Clear plastic bottles
- Blue food coloring
- Ice cubes
- Paper towels
- Markers and colored pencils
- Worksheets for note-taking and drawing

#### Lesson Outline:

**Introduction (10 minutes):**
- Begin with a brief discussion about water and its importance in our daily lives.
- Ask students if they know where water comes from and how it moves around the Earth.

**Direct Instruction (15 minutes):**
- Use the whiteboard to draw a simple diagram of the water cycle.
- Explain each stage of the water cycle: evaporation, condensation, precipitation, and collection.
- Highlight key terms and concepts, ensuring that students understand the sequence and processes involved.

**Interactive Activity (20 minutes):**
- Divide the class into small groups.
- Provide each group with a clear plastic bottle, blue food coloring, ice cubes, and paper towels.
- Instruct students to create a mini water cycle in their bottles by adding water and food coloring, then placing an ice cube on top.
- Observe and discuss the process as it occurs, linking it back to the larger water cycle.

**Guided Practice (15 minutes):**
- Distribute water cycle diagram handouts.
- Have students fill in the blanks and label each part of the water cycle.
- Walk around to assist and ensure understanding.

**Independent Work (15 minutes):**
- Students will complete a worksheet where they draw and label their own water cycle diagrams.
- Encourage creativity and accuracy in their illustrations.

**Closure (10 minutes):**
- Review the key points of the lesson.
- Ask students to share one thing they learned about the water cycle.
- Collect worksheets and diagrams for assessment.

#### Assessment:
- Observation during the interactive activity.
- Completion of the water cycle diagram and worksheet.
- Student participation in discussions and sharing.

#### Homework:
- Students can research and write a short paragraph about how human activities affect the water cycle.

This lesson plan is designed to be engaging and hands-on, allowing students to visualize and understand the complex processes of the water cycle through practical activities and visual aids.
